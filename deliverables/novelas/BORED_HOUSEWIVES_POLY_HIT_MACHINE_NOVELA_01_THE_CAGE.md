The house on the hill had seven windows facing the city. She had counted them every morning for three years. Seven windows. Three cars in the driveway. A husband who left at seven and returned at eight. A body she maintained like a second job. A life that looked, from the outside, like a win. From the inside it was a cage. Not a bad cage. A golden one. Nobody had hit her. Nobody had cheated—as far as she knew. Nobody had left. They had just stopped wanting. She had stopped wanting. Or maybe she had never been allowed to want the thing she actually wanted: the big energy. The one that didn't ask permission. The one that took. The one that made you feel like you were finally, finally alive.

She didn't call herself a housewife. She had a title at the foundation. She had a title at the club. She had a name that opened doors. Behind those doors was the same silence. The same smile. The same question she never said out loud: *Is this it?*

That morning the tablet was on the counter. A link. A friend had sent it. *For us,* the message said. *The ones in the cage. Press the button. You'll see.*

She almost didn't. She had deleted things like that before. Romance. Fantasy. Things that made her feel guilty for wanting. But the name at the top was different. **Bored Housewives Poly Hit Machine.** And under it: **Hero Freedom (Frida).** *Lift housewives out of their golden cages.* *Novelas. Click to see. Step in.*

She pressed the button.
